Island Life

(picture of yellow house, dark green tree, light green grass and a goat)

Bob and Rita live in a small house with a big mango tree and a patch of grass just outside the Valley. Rita works in the supermarket and Bob has a grill cart that he keeps mostly down around Sandy Ground and George Hill. Bob has two grown daughters: Sharon and Karen who live in the Valley. When the big cruise ship is in the harbor, Bob will take his cart down to Eddie's bar and cook up a mess of curry goat to sell to the guests. Sharon has a car that she drives as a taxi, so she'll usually give Bob a ride and pick up folks from the dock who want to tour the island or get a quick ride up to the Reggae Shack. Eddie's bar is pretty far up the beach from the dock, but Charlie the launch captain tells the guests that it's worth the walk for Bob's curry goat, and Eddie has a great place with tables in the shade and a good breeze.

In the summer when Rita's mangoes get ripe, they all get ripe within about 2 weeks. The tree is probably 80 years old and there are so many mangoes - she takes some to work, but mostly locals shop in the Valley supermarket, mangoes aren't anything special to them. Karen works in the hospital and she takes a couple of bags to share with her co-workers, but again they're mostly locals. Some of Rita's mangoes make it into local restaurants, and of course Bob offers sides of mango with his curry goat, and the guests rave about Rita's tree ripened mangoes when they get them, but the season for fresh tree ripened mango is very short.

Genesis

One slow October day Eddie, Bob and Gene were hanging out and Eddie was talking about the bankers trying to make their next billion using blockchain. Bob said: "I wish I had their problems, I just want EC$2700 so I can pay Dave to put an electric motor on my grill cart - if I could get up and down the hill easier, I'd sell a lot more curry goat." Gene is an old computer geek and he said: "you know, Bitcoin didn't start out as a billionaire's game, it got going as a grassroots project and grew up into what it is today - there are some styles of blockchain that still work well for smaller situations." Bob: "Yeah, how's that going to help me?" Gene: "Well, if you had your own blockchain and sold shares as promises for plates of curry goat, you could raise the money for your electric motor now, and deliver the goat later." Bob: "That sounds like too much contract and obligation for me, man. I sell my curry goat when it's ready, not on some schedule." Eddie: "Hey, I see how this could work. Bob doesn't need to promise the goat on any particular day, just that when somebody shows up at his grill cart and he's got curry goat ready, they can trade a pre-purchased promise for a plate of curry goat. Bob, I know you're almost always here when the Odyssey is in harbor, I'd front you EC$2000 against 100 plates - you move like 150 plates a month just at my place, right?" Bob: "yeah mon, you know I get EC$25 per plate at your place, too." Eddie: "I know, and I don't even ask for a cut because you help me sell drinks, but I also know when you roll that cart up to the top of George Hill, you're getting more like EC$10 per plate up there. Plus, I am fronting this money, there's some risk here even though I know you're good for it." Bob was still skeptical, but the more he thought about it the more sense it made.

Bob talked it over with Rita, and she was very excited about the idea. She invited Gene over and asked him how she might use blockchain to help her get her mangoes out to the resort restaurants when they are ripe, but not yet rotten. Gene thought for a minute and decided that Rita might make a conditional option the basis of her blockchain, so whoever holds mango credits in Rita's chain has the right to buy that amount of her available mangoes at a fixed price when they're ripe. Since the whole blockchain is open record, Rita can find people who are willing to pre-pay a little now to get her mangoes when they are ripe, and people considering purchasing mango options can easily assess whether or not Rita has over-subscribed her tree's likely crop for the year. The option price can be EC$1 for a small mango, EC$2 for a large, the supermarket charges a little more for mangoes, and the resorts will pay much more for good quality fruit. Each credit on Rita's blockchain is worth either one large or two small "mango options." It's all a little foreign to Bob and Rita, but Rita was enthused enough that she took a class at the community college in contracts and finance to learn more and maybe help her explain to people who might purchase mango options from her. For Rita, even if people pay almost nothing for her options, it gives her a way to find mango lovers when the fruit is ready.

ICO: Initial Curry Offering

Eddie makes good on his word and purchases 100 plates of Bob's curry goat for EC$2000. Eddie has a deal with Charlie already set up and he sells Charlie 50 plates for US$500. All that Bob and Gene know is that Eddie has split off 50 of his plates to someone else, since Charlie didn't identify himself in the transaction and he paid Eddie in cash. Charlie is happy because he has a similar system setup on other islands where guests going ashore give him some money up front and he gives them credits in an app in their phone. Then, when they are at participating places like Bob's grill cart, they can exchange their credits for services and goods like a plate of curry goat. Charlie is charging guests US$12 (EC$32.40) per plate of curry goat, but they're still happy with the system because they don't have to carry a lot of cash, and they can learn something about the places they are going to before they get there. For instance, Bob isn't 100% reliably available every time the Odyessy comes into harbor, but with the phone-app guests can check to see not only if he's there, but also how many plates of curry goat he has ready and his best guess of when he'll be closing up shop for the day. Bob has also completed some food safety and guest relations classes at the community college with great grades, and Gene linked evidence of these in Bob's blockchain which the guests' apps can verify against the college's open records, which include photos of a much younger Bob... So, now when they hit the dock and see Oscar's rusty grill right there, they can have more confidence spending their time walking up to Eddie's place to get the preferred curry goat plate from Bob, when he's around.

Free Market Forces

It isn't long before Bob gets word that his curry goat plates are selling for EC$32.40 through Charlie, so Bob raises his cash price to EC$35 while he's at Eddie's and the new guests all feel good about the "discount" that they are getting through Charlie and their phone-app.
*and besides, back home they might pay US$12 to US$16 for a similar entree in an ordinary restaurant

Charlie goes through his first 50 plates in a couple of stopovers, so he buys the other 50 from Eddie. Bob has the money for his electric motor now, so he goes to Dave to get it installed. Dave has heard about Bob's blockchain and wants to talk about it, and also buy some plates if Bob will sell them to him. So, Bob pays Dave EC$1500 cash, and gives him 60 plates for the EC$1200 balance, keeping the EC$1200 he thought he was going to be spending that day. Bob's not crazy about owing so much goat-debt, but so far it's not cramping his style.

Seasonal variation

In April the big cruise ship heads back to Europe and Bob's lucrative business slows down. Thanks to AOE (and Ziggy) he's getting more guests out of Blowing Point now, but instead of selling 200 plates a month at guest prices, now he's selling more like 100. Rita warned him about this and told him to reduce his shares outstanding, but Bob didn't listen, so now his investors aren't getting return for almost twice as long as they were in-season. Some take it well and just wait, but others drop their ask prices down to what they paid or even lower after they don't get paid off in a couple of months. It's a learning process, next year will be better, and meanwhile Bob still has his cash business.

For Rita, mango season is coming up soon. Rita estimates that she harvests about 250 large mangoes a year, and she's only sold about 100 options so far, a lot of work for not much return, but what it's really about for Rita is opening a market that didn't exist before. In June the fruit starts falling, and Rita sets out with Sharon to go calling on their mango option holders. Some people politely purchase the mangoes they have options for (after all, EC$2 is a really good price for one of Rita's tree ripe Hayden mangoes), others decline for whatever reasons, but a couple of the resort restaurants start absolutely raving about how much better Rita's mangoes are than the ones they get from the grocery. They exercise all their options and purchase the rest of Rita's stock for the summer at EC$6 per large mango.

A Web of Trust

Trust starts between people who know each other well in real day to day life. When these people do open business on a blockchain for a number of years, the patterns shown on their blockchain can start to develop some implicit trust by people who may not know them as well. Of course, histories can be manufactured and faked - to a point, which is why the Web of Trust becomes important - genuine should not be endorsing fakes or frauds, and if they do they seriously diminish their own "trust value" in the web. Part of Bob's published identity on his blockchain includes links to his completed courses at the community college. The community college is a well established and trusted entity
*Of course an entity like a college can be subject to some records faking and fraud by people within the organization, but the organization should be policing itself to keep its records clean and trustable.
and through it's publicly exposed records, it vouches for the fact that Bob completed those classes and provides some additional identity confirmation like Bob's student ID photo. Although some share transfer transactions can be obscured by cryptographic keys without attached identities, others can be confirmed with trusted identities. Cash traders like Charlie, Ted and even Ziggy include their identity on many transactions, enabling their clients to establish increased trust with the vendors that they vouch for. Like when Alice trusted the Rainforest tour company because Ted vouched for them, Alice didn't know Ted, but Alice knew Charlie and she knew that Charlie trusted Ted and that Ted trusted the Rainforest people, so Alice took a very small risk in trusting the Rainforest people with her afternoon's entertainment. Multiple degrees of separation is not the kind of trust you want when investing your life's savings, but for small low risk things it can be much better than simply trusting that the local government hasn't shut an operation down, yet, or online reviews of dubious origins.

As hundreds or thousands of small participants start to keep open and (at least somewhat) reliable records on blockchains, the Web of Trust that develops can support a wide variety of low risk interactions that would have previously been inadvisable due to the almost complete lack of information available about strangers in unregulated businesses.
